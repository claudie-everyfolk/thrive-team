# Exit Interview Themes — Q1 2026

**Prepared by:** People Analytics
**Period covered:** Departures with exit date between 2026-01-01 and 2026-03-31
**Total exits in scope:** 38 (voluntary only; involuntary excluded)
**Method:** Structured exit interview + free-response coded into themes. Reported here as themes with weight (count of exits citing the theme) and representative paraphrases. Quotes lightly edited for anonymity.

---

## Customer Operations (n=14 exits)

**Headline:** Highest voluntary attrition of any department this quarter. Up from 8 in Q4 2025.

### Themes (ranked by frequency)

1. **Workload outpacing staffing (cited in 11 of 14)** — Volume growth is described as compounding while headcount has been flat for ~3 quarters. Several exits cited mandatory overtime and weekend coverage as the breaking point.
2. **Schedule unpredictability (cited in 8 of 14)** — Multiple exits, especially those with caregiving responsibilities, said published-on-Saturday schedules made the role incompatible with their lives.
3. **Manager bandwidth (cited in 7 of 14)** — Pattern of skipped 1:1s and managers being pulled into queue coverage. ICs felt unsupported through escalations and unclear on career path.
4. **Unclear promotion criteria (cited in 5 of 14)** — Common framing: "I watched people promoted around me and no one ever told me what was missing."
5. **Recent VP hire (cited in 4 of 14)** — Two internal candidates were passed over; both have since left. Exits framed this as a signal about how the department is valued.

### Notable destinations

7 of 14 went to roles at competitors or peer-stage e-commerce companies in similar functions, with reported comp uplift of 10-20%. 3 left the industry entirely.

## Engineering (n=9 exits)

**Headline:** Flat vs Q4 2025 (8 exits). Concentrated on the platform team.

### Themes (ranked by frequency)

1. **On-call burden on the platform team (cited in 6 of 9; 5 of 6 platform-team exits)** — Rotation of 5 covering services that arguably need 8+. Burnout cited as primary driver.
2. **Roadmap volatility (cited in 5 of 9)** — Engineers described shipping the same area two or three times after re-prioritization. Felt their work was disposable.
3. **Career path beyond Staff (cited in 4 of 9)** — Senior ICs reported no clear track for what comes after Staff Engineer. Promotion conversations stalled.
4. **Hiring bar perception (cited in 3 of 9)** — Tenured engineers noted a perceived dip in new-hire quality and felt code review was carrying the gap.

### What's working (notable)

Onboarding, interview process, and compensation transparency came up as positives in exit conversations — multiple departing engineers explicitly praised them. The leakage is not at the entry point.

### Notable destinations

5 of 9 went to platform/infra roles at other companies. 2 went to AI/ML focused startups. 2 to FAANG-tier comp jumps (>40%).

---

## Merchandising (n=8 exits)

**Headline:** Slightly elevated vs Q4 (6). Concentrated in buyer roles.

### Themes (ranked by frequency)

1. **Compensation gap vs market (cited in 7 of 8)** — Buyer comp described as 15-25% below external offers received. Two exits explicitly named raising it internally and being told to "revisit at year-end."
2. **Tooling friction (cited in 5 of 8)** — Heavy reliance on spreadsheets and aging internal tools. Several exits said this was a quality-of-life issue but became a deciding factor when combined with comp.
3. **Internal mobility frustration (cited in 4 of 8)** — Pattern of applying for internal roles, never hearing back, and the role being filled externally. Eroded trust in the company's growth narrative.
4. **Title vs scope misalignment (cited in 3 of 8)** — Specifically: associate buyers doing senior buyer work without title or pay adjustment.

### Notable destinations

6 of 8 went to other retail / e-commerce buyer roles with reported comp uplift of 15-22%. 1 to a CPG brand-side role. 1 retired.

---

## Cross-cutting observations (all departments)

- **Career-development conversations are absent or transactional** across all three departments. ICs reporting "I get my review, I get my comp letter, that's it" appears in every function.
- **Internal mobility friction** shows up in Merchandising and Engineering. The Customer Operations VP search is a related signal — internal candidates passed over.
- **Manager bandwidth** is the connective tissue: when managers are stretched, the symptoms differ by department (queue coverage in CO, alignment meetings in Eng, cross-functional firefighting in Merch), but the IC-side experience is the same — less coaching, less time, less support.
- **Compensation issues are concentrated** in Customer Operations (workload-adjusted) and Merchandising (band-vs-market). Engineering compensation is widely praised in exits.

---

## Recommended HRBP follow-ups

- **Customer Operations:** the staffing model is the root cause, not the symptom. Coaching, training, and engagement programs will not move the needle until workload-per-IC is addressed.
- **Engineering:** the platform on-call rotation is a localized but severe leak. Solvable with targeted action; not a department-wide engagement issue.
- **Merchandising:** the comp band review should not wait for year-end. Each lost senior buyer is being replaced with a junior, which compounds the issue.
